Six Sigma Project
June 2024
Executive Summary

The project was undertaken to address high defect rates in the stamping process of LS-331C, with the following key goals:

  • Reduce defects from 8% to below 1%.
  • Improve process capability from Cpk = 0.73 to ≥1.33.
  • Save $190,000 annually in rework and scrap costs.
    •
Approach

Using the DMAIC (Define-Measure-Analyze-Improve-Control) methodology, the team applied advanced Six Sigma Black Belt tools to identify and mitigate the root causes of defects.

  1. Define Phase
    • Created a project charter to define objectives, scope, and deliverables.
    • Mapped the high-level process using a SIPOC diagram.
    • Established CTQs, focusing on defect-free parts and process stability.
  2. Measure Phase
    • Conducted a Pareto Analysis to prioritize defect types (65% cracks, 35% splits)
    • Baseline performance metrics showed a defect rate of 8% and Cpk = 0.73.
    • Measurement system analysis (Gauge R&R) indicated 12% variability, necessitating improved measurement processes
  3. Analyze Phase
    4. Root Cause Analysis using a Fishbone Diagram identified key factors :-
    • Material thickness variability.
    • Die misalignment during setups.
    • Inconsistent lubrication application.
    • Insufficient operator training.
    • Statistical tools, including regression analysis, confirmed a strong correlation (R² = 0.84) between material variability and defects.
    • FMEA highlighted material thickness as the highest-risk factor, with an RPN of 270.
  4. Improve Phase
    5. Implemented solutions :-
    • Updated supplier specifications to reduce material thickness variability (±0.05 mm).
    • Installed die alignment sensors and upgraded to automated lubrication dispensers.
    • Conducted targeted operator training.
    • Performed a Design of Experiments (DOE) to determine optimal press force and lubrication levels.
    • Piloted improvements on a single line, reducing defects to 0.9%.
  5. Control Phase
    • Introduced Statistical Process Control (SPC) to monitor key variables.
    • Updated Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) and trained operators on process changes.
    • Scheduled monthly audits for die alignment and lubrication system maintenance.
    • Implemented Poka-Yoke mechanisms, such as visual die alignment markers, to prevent recurrence.
Results
  • Defect Rate: Reduced from 8% to 0.7%.
  • Financial Savings: Achieved annual savings of $190,000.
  • Customer Satisfaction: Reduced complaints by 95%.
  • Process Capability: Improved from Cpk = 0.73 to 1.45.
  • Machine Downtime: Reduced by 30%.
Key Technical Tools Utilized
  • DMAIC Methodology: Framework for structured problem-solving.
  • Pareto Analysis: Prioritizing defect types.
  • SPC Charts: Monitoring process stability.
  • Gauge R&R: Measurement system evaluation.
  • FMEA: Risk analysis and mitigation.
  • Regression Analysis: Identifying critical relationships between variables.
  • DOE: Optimizing process parameters.
  • Poka-Yoke: Error-proofing solutions.

The project demonstrated the effectiveness of Six Sigma principles in resolving manufacturing challenges, delivering significant quality and financial benefits while ensuring long-term process stability.

Notable 8D
December 2024

Executive Summary

A thorough breakdown of each step in the 8D process with added explanations, data-driven actions, and Six Sigma tools for precise root cause identification and resolution. It incorporates both a structured root cause analysis and the corresponding corrective actions.

Step 1 : Team Formation (D1)

Objective : Assemble a cross-functional team to address the flatness issue.

Team Members :-
  • Quality Engineer (Team Lead)
  • Production Supervisor
  • Maintenance Technician
  • Process Engineer
  • Operator Representative
  • Customer Quality Representative
Why: A diverse team ensures a holistic approach to identifying and resolving the issue.
Step 2 : Problem Description (D2)

Problem Statement : A stamped automotive part exhibits flatness deviations. Customers reported assembly difficulties, resulting in a complaint.

Specifications :-
  • Flatness Tolerance: Not defined on the drawing.
  • Observed Deviation: Up to 0.68 mm.
  • Defect Rate: 8% of inspected parts exceeded acceptable flatness during the customer’s audit.
Impact :-
  • Assembly delays on the customer line.
  • Potential contractual penalties
Step 3 : Team Formation (D3)
Actions Taken :-
  • All production halted temporarily.
  • 100% inspection initiated for flatness using a custom flatness gauge.
  • Defective parts segregated and labeled as non-conforming.
  • Customer informed about the containment plan.
  • Outcome: Customer production resumed using inspected conforming parts.
Step 4 : Team Formation (D3)
Tools Used:-
  • Fishbone Diagram : Identified potential causes under categories like Man, Machine, Material, Method and Measurement
  • 5 Whys Analysis : Drilled down to identify why the problem occurred and why it was not detected.
Identified Causes :-
  1. Why Made :-
    • Straightener rollers were misaligned.
    • Straightener settings were not optimized for material thickness.
  2. Why Not Detected :-
    • Engineering drawing lacked a flatness callout.
    • Inspection instructions for the fixture did not include flatness checks.
    • Operators were unaware of flatness requirements.
  3. Systemic Gaps :-
    • No flatness gauge was available for routine inspection.
    • FMEA did not identify flatness as a critical quality characteristic.
Step 5 : Permanent Corrective Actions (D5)
Machine :-
  • Calibrated and aligned straightener rollers
  • Optimized straightener settings using a Design of Experiments (DOE) approach to determine the best parameters.
Provess :-
  • Updated engineering drawings to include a flatness tolerance of ±0.25 mm.
  • Added flatness checks using a flatness gauge at the inspection station.
People
  • Conducted operator training on flatness requirements.
  • Implemented standardized work instructions and visual aids for flatness checks.
Documentation
  • Updated FMEA, Control Plan, and Inspection Instructions to include flatness checks as a critical step.
Step 6 : Verification of Corrective Actions (D6)
  • Process Capabaility Study : Collected flatness measurements over 12 weeks.
  • Pre-Correction Defect Rate : 8%
  • Post-COrrection Defect Rate :Reduced to 0.1%
  • Process Capability Index (cPK) : Improved from 0.8 to 1.5
  • Effectivveness Check : Customer audit verified that all supplied parts met flatness requirements.
  • Internal audits confirmed adherence to the updated process.
Step 7 : Prevent Recurrence (D7)
Systemic CHanges :-
  • Updated FMEA to highlight flatness as a high-priority characteristic.
  • Implemented regular straightener calibration and maintenance schedules.
  • Introduced a flatness reference plate at the inspection fixture to streamline checks.
Operator Awarness :-
  • Conducted monthly refresher training sessions.
  • Posted updated control plans and inspection sheets at workstations.
Step 8 : Team Recognition (D8)
Acknowledgment :-
  • Celebrated team success with a recognition event.
  • Highlighted individual contributions during a company-wide meeting.
Documentation :-
  • Compiled a case study to share best practices across other departments.

Key Metrics and Results

  • Before Correction:
    • Flatness Defect Rate: 8%.
    • Cpk: 0.8.
  • >After Correction:
    • Flatness Defect Rate: 0.1%.
    • Cpk: 1.5.
    • Customer Satisfaction: Improved; no complaints reported in the following quarter.

Visual Aids and Templates (Explanation Only)

  • Fishbone Diagram: Displays categorized potential causes.
  • SPC Chart: Monitors weekly flatness variations and shows process improvement trends.
  • FMEA Template: Updated to include flatness as a critical failure mode.
  • Control Plan: Ensures all steps are standardized for quality assurance.

This approach demonstrates thorough application of the 8D methodology, with detailed steps, systemic improvements, and data-driven validations.

Lean Project 1
Sep to Dec 2018
Problem

Low Production Rate in Injection Molding Process

Observation

At QM Plastics, the production rate was significantly lower than expected. The OEE was measured at 72%, indicating inefficiencies across three critical components of OEE: availability, performance, and quality.

Key Issues Identified
  1. High Changeover Time :-
    • Prolonged die changes during setup caused machine idle time.
    • Lack of proper sequencing and staging for dies, tools, and materials.
  2. Poor Standardized Work :-
    • Operators followed inconsistent procedures.
    • Tasks were not clearly documented, leading to variability in execution.
  3. Non-Value-Added Tasks :-
    • Multiple redundant activities were observed during the die change and operation processes.
  4. No Structured System for Quality Checks :-
    • Sampling inspections for raw materials and finished goods were irregular, increasing rejection rates.

Solution : Lean Implementation to Address Inefficiencies

Step 1: Problem Understanding through Current State Analysis
Value Stream Mapping (VSM) :-
  • Analyzed the process flow to pinpoint bottlenecks during the setup and operation phases.
  • Identified a delay of 20-30 minutes per changeover due to manual die alignment and insufficient material staging.
MODAPTS Time Study :-
  • Decomposed tasks into measurable units to assess inefficiencies.
  • Found that nearly 32% of the total time spent during setup was non-value-added.
Step 2: Applying Lean Tools to Reduce Changeover Time
Single Minute Exchange of Dies (SMED) :-
  • Single Minute Exchange of Dies (SMED):
    • Categorized setup tasks into:
      • Internal tasks: Those requiring the machine to be idle.
      • External tasks: Those that could be performed while the machine was running.
    • Shifted tasks such as material staging and die cleaning from internal to external, saving up to 25% of the total setup time.
  • New Die Staging Process:
    • Created a pre-staging checklist for operators to ensure all tools and dies were prepared in advance.
    • Introduced quick-release clamps to speed up die alignment.
Step 3 Eliminating Non-Value-Added Tasks
Single Minute Exchange of Dies (SMED) :-
  • Yamazumi Chart (Workload Balancing):
    • Identified redundant steps such as double-checking already staged materials.
    • Reassigned tasks to reduce overlapping responsibilities among operators.
  • Process Simplification:
    • Removed unnecessary tasks, including excessive cleaning of tools already inspected in prior stages.
Step 4: Improving Workforce Efficiency
  • Standardized Work Documentation:
    • Documented step-by-step procedures for setup and operation tasks.
    • Conducted training sessions for operators to ensure consistent adherence to the new process.
  • Visual Aids and Job Aids:
    • Added visual cues such as diagrams and workflows near machines for quick reference.
Step 5: Enhancing Quality Inspection
  • Recommended hiring an ASQ Certified Quality Auditor for systematic quality checks.
  • Developed a sampling plan for raw materials and finished goods to reduce defect rates and improve first-pass yield.
Step 6: Monitoring and Validation
  • Conducted a pilot run with the new changes to evaluate effectiveness:
    • Setup time was reduced by 32%.
    • Machine idle time during die changes decreased from 30 minutes to 18 minutes.
  • Measured the OEE components post-implementation:
    • Availability: Improved from 85% to 90%.
    • Performance: Increased from 80% to 85%.
    • Quality: Enhanced from 90% to 93%.
Final Results
  • The OEE improved from 72% to 79%, exceeding the industry benchmark.
  • Achieved a sustainable reduction in changeover times.
  • Established a culture of continuous improvement by implementing standardized processes and regular training.

LEAN Project 2
Jan To April 2019
Problem

Productivity Loss on Press 1184

Observation

Press 1184 exhibited significant productivity loss due to inefficiencies in material handling, prolonged changeover times, and lack of standardized processes.

Key Metrics
  • Baseline Changeover Time : 143 minutes.
  • Operator idle time and uncoordinated communication further delayed operations
Task

Analyze and Resolve Productivity Issues

Goal

Reduce changeover time, establish standardized processes, and improve overall productivity.

Approach
  • Utilize 5W1H (Who, What, Where, When, Why, How) to understand the problem comprehensively
  • Perform a 5WHY Analysis to uncover the root cause.
  • Apply Lean tools like 5S, SMED, and implement an ANDON system to enhance efficiency and communication.
Actions
  1. Identifying the Problem with 5W1H :-
    • What? Press 1184 has unacceptably high changeover times.
    • Who? Operators lack a clear system to follow for loading/unloading materials.
    • Where? Press operation and staging area for materials.
    • When? Changeovers typically occur during shifts, with significant delays.
    • Why? Inconsistent processes and no standardized work instructions.
    • How? Changeovers involve unnecessary movements, delays, and redundant tasks.
  2. Root Cause Analysis Using 5WHY :-
    3. Why? Answer
    Why is the changeover taking so long? Excessive manual adjustments during die change.
    Why are manual adjustments necessary? Operators don't follow a standardized process.
    Why is there no standardized process? Lack of documented instructions and operator training.
    Why hasn't this been addressed before? No system in place to highlight delays or track changeover times.
    Why is communication lacking? No real-time alert system to inform management about delays.
  3. Lean Tools and Implementation :-
    • 5S on Press 1184:
      • Sorted tools and materials to eliminate unnecessary items.
      • Created a shadow board for tools near the press to minimize operator movement.
      • Ensured systematic cleaning and maintenance schedules.
    • Single Minute Exchange of Dies (SMED):
      • Segregated internal (machine idle) and external (machine running) tasks.
      • Pre-staged materials and tools before the machine was idle.
      • Implemented quick-change clamps to simplify die alignment.
    • Standardized Work Documentation:
      • Created a step-by-step guide for operators, reducing variability.
      • Conducted operator training sessions to ensure adherence.
    • Suggested ANDON System:
      • Introduced a real-time communication system to alert supervisors and technicians of delays or issues immediately.
    • Process Monitoring and Follow-Up:
      • Conducted periodic audits every three weeks to ensure processes were followed.
Results

Verified Improvements

  1. Changeover Time Reduction:
    • Reduced from 143 minutes to 91 minutes (36% improvement).
  2. Productivity Boost:
    • Increased press uptime and throughput by 25%.
  3. Operator Efficiency:
    • Reduced unnecessary movements and manual adjustments by 40%.
  4. Improved Communication:
    • Reduced downtime caused by delayed troubleshooting by implementing ANDON.

Lean Project 3
April to July 2019
Problem

The production process at the facility had significant inefficiencies, reflected in a low Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE) of 70%. Key issues included :

  • Motion Waste: Workers spent excessive time walking between tasks, leading to productivity loss.
  • Overburden: Certain workstations were overloaded, while others faced idle time.
  • Defects: A recurring quality issue in one process resulted in rework and material waste.
Task

The goal was to :-

  1. Map the process to identify inefficiencies and wastes.
  2. Reconfigure the production flow to reduce motion and overburden.
  3. Implement measures to eliminate defects and improve quality.
  4. Increase OEE from 70% to at least 80%.
Action Taken
  1. Process Analysis Using Value Stream Mapping (VSM)
    • Conducted a detailed study of the current production state.
    • Identified key areas contributing to waste:
      • Motion Waste: Workers covered an average distance of 850 meters per shift, resulting in delays.
      • Overburden: Station B experienced 30% higher cycle time than other stations.
      • Defects: Affected 12 out of every 100 parts produced, translating to a defect rate of 12%.
  2. Implementation of Cell Manufacturing
    • Redesigned the layout into a U-shaped cell structure:
      • Reduced average worker motion by 300 meters per shift (a 35% reduction).
      • Increased production flow, enabling continuous movement of workpieces.
      • Conducted ergonomic studies to optimize workstation arrangement.
  3. Work Distribution and Cell Balancing
    • Analyzed cycle times across all stations:
      • Average cycle time reduced from 45 seconds to 37 seconds (an 18% improvement).
    • Introduced task realignment to balance the load:
      • Station B’s workload reduced by 25%, aligning it with other stations.
      • Utilized a Yamazumi Chart to visualize and rebalance tasks.
  4. Kaizen for Quality Improvement
    • Conducted root cause analysis using a 5 Whys approach for defects.
    • Standardized procedures and introduced real-time quality checks.
    • Implemented a poka-yoke (error-proofing) mechanism to prevent common errors.
Results
  1. Improved Motion Efficiency
    • Worker movement reduced by 35% (from 850 meters to 550 meters per shift), saving an estimated 20 minutes per shift.
  2. Enhanced OEE
    • Increased from 70% to 82%, achieving a productivity gain of 12%.
  3. Defect Reduction
    • Defect rate reduced from 12% to 9%, saving approximately 30 reworks per day for a batch of 1,000 parts.
  4. Cycle Time Efficiency
    • Average cycle time reduced by 8 seconds, enabling the production of an additional 25 parts per shift.
Quantified Impact
  • Time Saved : 20 minutes per shift = 10 hours/month for the entire team
  • Cost Savings from Defects : Estimated savings of $2,500/month due to reduced rework.
  • Increased Output : Additional 500 parts produced per month.
Quantified Impact

Production Optimization Using VSM, Cell Manufacturing, and Kaizen

Optimized a production process with a low OEE of 70% by analyzing and addressing inefficiencies through Value Stream Mapping (VSM). Reduced worker motion by 35% and balanced workloads across stations using Yamazumi Charts. Improved cycle time efficiency by 18%, enabling an additional 500 parts per month. Applied Kaizen to reduce defect rates by 25%, saving $2,500 in monthly rework costs. These initiatives elevated OEE from 70% to 82%, creating a more streamlined and cost-effective operation.

Problem

Stroke patients often face severe disabilities, particularly in the hands, fingers, and wrists, which limit their ability to perform daily tasks

  • Traditional rehabilitation methods rely heavily on physiotherapists, which can be expensive and time-consuming.
  • Many patients, especially in rural areas, lack access to continuous professional care.
  • Existing machines for rehabilitation are costly and stationary, making them inaccessible for home use.
Task

The objective was to design and fabricate a cost-effective, portable, and automated rehabilitation machine that :-

  1. Enables stroke patients to perform passive exercises independently.
  2. Focuses on key affected areas: hands, fingers, and wrists.
  3. Offers affordability and portability to reach a broader audience.
  4. Serves as a foundation for future expansion to treat leg disabilities.
Actions Taken
  1. Problem Understanding and Research

  4. Future Scope and Expansion

    • Documented the project for further collaboration with medical professionals and potential commercialization.
Results
  1. Accessibility and Affordability
